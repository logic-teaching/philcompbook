Problem 1#

This is practice with the untyped lambda calculus from Chapter 5.

This problem has three parts.

First, the associativity conventions dictate that \(PQR\) and \(OP(QR)\) are abbreviations for terms with at one extra pair of parentheses. Indicate what these terms are.

Second, give an intuitive example where \(PQR\) means something different than \(P(QR)\). Your answer can just be a full English sentence with three intuitive functions \(P,Q,R\) which you can describe in prose (usually intuitive functions are implicitly typed, but just set that aside for the moment).

Third consider Bluebird \({\mathbf B}\): \(\lambda xyz.x(yz)\) and do two things with it.

  • Replace all free and bound instances of \(x,y,z\) by \(u,v,w\) respectively to form the equivalent \({\mathbf B}^{\prime}\): \(\lambda uvw.u(vw)\) (these are equivalent in the same way that formulas with renamed bound variables are equivalent).

  • Show that \({\mathbf B}{\mathbf B}^{\prime}\triangleright \lambda yzvw . yz(vw)\).

Optionally, you may replace blackbird by any other bird from the list. That is, you choose a bird (a row), and prove that the term in the fourth column beta reduces to the lambda expression in the first column.

Problem 2#

This practice with the typed lambda calculus from Chapter 6.

Consider reviewing the typing of Kestral and Starling before beginning.

This problem has three parts:

First, suppose that \(x:B\rightarrow C\) and \(y:A\rightarrow B\) and \(z:A\). Show that Bluebird \({\mathbf B}\) can be typed as \({\mathbf B}:(B\rightarrow C)\rightarrow ((A\rightarrow B)\rightarrow (A\rightarrow C))\)

The idea of the Curry-Howard isomorphism is that the types corresponds to propositions and that showing that an expression has a given type is the same as showing that the corresponding proposition has a proof.

Second, write down a proof of the tautology \((B\rightarrow C)\rightarrow ((A\rightarrow B)\rightarrow (A\rightarrow C))\) (it should involve two applications of modus ponens). In 1-2 complete English sentences, describe whether intuitively you would take this proof to be the same as your earlier proof of the typing of bluebird.

Third, indicate how when \(x:B\rightarrow C\) and \(y:A\rightarrow B\) are considered as functions, then \({\mathbf B}xy\) is the natural formula for the composition from \(A\) to \(C\). Optionally draw a picture of the composition.

Finally, optionally, you may replace bluebird by any other bird from the list

Problem 4#

This is a problem related to Chapter 8 and Gödel [Gödel, 1995].

Consider a theory \(T\) to which Gödel’s Second Incompleteness Theorem applies i.e. it satisfies conditions 1-3 in our description of the theorem.

Let \(T^{+}\) be \(T\) plus \(\mathrm{Con}(T)\), and let \(T^{-}\) be \(T\) plus \(\neg \mathrm{Con}(T)\), so that both \(T^+\) and \(T^-\) are deductively consistent.

In one paragraph, explain why

  1. \(T^+\) satisfies each of conditions 1-3

  2. \(T^-\) fails to satisfy exactly one of conditions 1-3

Note: this again underscores a deep difference between Gödel’s First Incompleteness Theorem and Gödel’s Second Incompleteness Theorem, since it shows that we can repeatedly bifrucate our theories in the context of the First Incompleteness Theorem but not in the context of the Second Incompleteness Theorem.

Hint: for 2, use a process of elimination to help you get started.

Problem 6#

This is a problem related to Chapter 10.

This problem has two parts.

First, draw a picture of \(\mathbb{N}_{\bot}\).

Second, show that if-then-else is continuous.

Note 1: we drew the picture in lecture. If you don’t take a moment and do this, experience suggests that it is easy to get mixed up and think that the order on \(\mathbb{N}_{\bot}\) involves the order \(0\leq 1\leq 2\leq \cdots\) on the natural numbers.

Note 2: for a function \(f(x,y,z)\) with three inputs, the continuity conditions are:

  1. Monotonicity: \(d_1\sqsubseteq d_1^{\prime}, d_2\sqsubseteq d_2^{\prime}, d_3\sqsubseteq d_3^{\prime}\) implies \(f(d_1, d_2, d_3)\sqsubseteq f(d_1^{\prime}, d_2^{\prime}, d_3^{\prime})\)

  2. Continuity: \(d_{1}\sqsubseteq d_{2}\sqsubseteq d_{3}\sqsubseteq \cdots\) and \(d_{1}^{\prime}\sqsubseteq d_{2}^{\prime}\sqsubseteq d_{3}^{\prime}\sqsubseteq \cdots\) and \(d_{1}^{\prime\prime}\sqsubseteq d_{2}^{\prime\prime}\sqsubseteq d_{3}^{\prime\prime}\sqsubseteq \cdots\) implies \(\bigsqcup_{n>0} f(d_{n}, d_n^{\prime}, d_n^{\prime\prime})=f(\bigsqcup_{n>0} d_{n}, \bigsqcup_{n>0} d_{n}^{\prime}, \bigsqcup_{n>0} d_{n}^{\prime\prime})\)
